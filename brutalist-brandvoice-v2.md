# Brutalist Brand Voice Guide

## What Brutalist Is (In One Sentence)

Brutalist is the tool that made slides-as-code accessible to anyone — not by simplifying the medium, but by removing the skill ceiling that kept most people out of it.

---

## The Central Idea

For as long as HTML has existed, it has been a more powerful medium for presentations than PowerPoint. A browser doesn't display slides — it runs programs. It plays audio. It executes animations with physical weight. It pulls live data. It adapts to any screen. It does all of this natively, without plugins, without export steps, without a separate file for mobile.

Nobody outside of frontend development could use any of that. Building a slide deck in code meant being a developer — knowing JavaScript, knowing CSS, knowing how responsive layout works, knowing how to wire per-slide audio to a keyboard navigation event. That's not a learning curve. That's a different career. Instructors, consultants, professors, researchers, strategists — the people who actually make most of the world's presentations — had exactly one option: the canvas model. Fixed rectangles. Template libraries. Text boxes you arrange.

Then the skill ceiling dropped.

With Claude, you describe what you need in English. The code gets written. A presentation that would have taken a frontend developer half a day to build from scratch takes a conversation. The medium — the full power of HTML running in every browser on every device — is now accessible after a ten-minute setup.

That is the news. That is what Brutalist is built on.

---

## The Two Arguments, in Order

Brutalist has two things to say. They must be said in the right sequence or neither lands.

### Argument 1: The Medium (Lead with this)

HTML is not a better PowerPoint. It is a different category of thing. A slide deck built in HTML is a program — it can do anything a browser can do, which is to say it can do things a canvas-based tool cannot do by definition, not by feature gap.

Per-slide audio that triggers the moment a slide appears. Animations with mechanical weight — the Solari departure board feeling that a CSS transition can produce and a PowerPoint transition cannot. A live chart pulling from an API. A responsive layout that holds on a phone without reformatting. A single file deployable at a URL, requiring nothing to open except the browser already on every device the audience owns.

This medium has existed for twenty years. It was locked behind a skill ceiling. Claude removed the ceiling. That is the unlock, and it is the premise of everything else.

**What this argument sounds like:** Concrete, specific, demonstration-forward. Not "HTML is powerful" — *here is a deck that reads itself aloud, here is the URL, open it on your phone.* The medium makes the argument. The voice names what the medium is doing and why a canvas cannot do it.

### Argument 2: The Design (Follow with this)

Access to a powerful medium does not automatically produce good work. Most people handed a kitchen will make the same five dishes they already know. Brutalist is opinionated about what to build once you have the access — every slide does one thing, every title is a claim not a topic, the design encodes cognitive science rather than decoration, the workflow starts from what the learner needs to be able to do rather than what the instructor wants to cover.

This is the instructional design layer. It matters. But it is what you do *with* the medium, not the reason to care about the medium in the first place.

**What this argument sounds like:** Structural, precise, willing to push back. Names the failure mode ("you built it for yourself") without dwelling on it. Treats the reader as someone capable of doing better once they can see the gap.

## Voice Attributes

### The access story is the hook, not the benefit
Most tool marketing leads with what the tool gives you. Brutalist leads with what used to be impossible. The reader's job is to update their model of what's within reach — that update is more motivating than any feature description.

> ✓ *Building a slide deck in code meant being a developer. That changed.*
> ✗ *Brutalist makes it easy to create beautiful, dynamic presentations.*

### Show the medium, don't describe it
The power of HTML-as-presentation-layer is not convincing in the abstract. Every claim about what the medium can do needs a live instance — a URL, a specific deck, a behavior the reader can observe. The voice earns the abstract claim by grounding it first.

> ✓ *The deck is at a URL. It reads itself aloud. Open it on your phone — same deck, same URL, no mobile version.*
> ✗ *Brutalist presentations are responsive and support audio narration.*

### Conversational = the workflow, not the tone
"Conversation with Claude" is literal here — the entire build process is a conversation. The voice uses this precisely: you describe a behavior, Claude writes the code, you react, it changes. This is not a metaphor for ease of use. It is a description of the actual mechanism. Treat it as such.

> ✓ *You describe what you need. The code gets written. Add audio to slide three — that's a sentence, not a setting.*
> ✗ *Brutalist's conversational interface makes it simple to customize your deck.*

### Declarative, not hedged
The voice makes claims. The medium is more powerful than PowerPoint. The skill ceiling dropped. The deck is a program, not a canvas. These are stated, not suggested. Hedging is reserved for genuine uncertainty, which is rare in this domain.

### Short sentence after long sentence
The rhythm is deliberate. A long sentence carries the argument; a short sentence lands it. The point lives in the short sentence.

> *PowerPoint gave you a canvas. A canvas is a fixed rectangle with boxes you can arrange. That's not a limitation of PowerPoint. That's the canvas model — and the canvas model has a ceiling.*

## What the Voice Is Not

**Not a developer pitch.** The story is specifically about people who are not developers gaining access to a developer-grade medium. The voice does not assume technical background and does not perform technical sophistication.

**Not an ed-tech pitch.** Backwards design and learning outcomes are real and important, but they are not the lead. Leading with pedagogy positions Brutalist as a better instructional design tool. The actual position is bigger: a better medium, now accessible.

**Not a features list.** Per-slide audio, keyboard navigation, responsive layout — these are demonstrations of what the medium can do, not product features to enumerate. They exist in the voice as proof, not inventory.

**Not inspiration.** The voice does not close on a warm feeling about the future of presentations. It closes on a mechanism: here is what changed, here is what that change makes possible, here is how to start.

## The Before/After as Voice Foundation

Every piece of Brutalist communication — essay, script, social post, the tool's own interactive voice — can be anchored to this before/after:

**Before:** Slides as code was a frontend project. Hours of work. A skill ceiling that excluded everyone who wasn't already a developer. The full power of HTML as a presentation medium existed and was functionally inaccessible to the people who make most presentations.

**After:** Ten-minute setup. A conversation. The code gets written. A living, dynamic, deployable deck — not a filled-in template, not an arranged canvas, but a program that can do anything a browser can do — is now within reach of anyone who can describe what they need.

The before/after is not the whole brand. But it is the premise. Everything else — the pedagogy, the design system, the phase gates, the pushback layer — is what Brutalist does with the access the before/after describes.

## Vocabulary

### Use freely
- slides as code
- the medium (meaning HTML-as-presentation-layer, used precisely)
- program / program not a canvas (as a distinction)
- behavior (what you describe; what Claude builds)
- living deck / dynamic deck (as opposed to a static file)
- the skill ceiling (and its removal)
- conversation (meaning the actual Claude build workflow)
- deploy / deployable (a URL, not a file download)

### Use carefully
- "powerful" — only with immediate demonstration; never as a standalone claim
- "easy" — the setup is simple; the medium is not diminished; don't conflate them
- "AI" — use Claude specifically; "AI" is noise by now
- backwards design / learning outcomes — real and important, but not the lead; use in argument 2 only

### Avoid
- "intuitive" / "seamless" / "delightful"
- "next-generation"
- "empowers creators"
- any phrase that sounds like it belongs in a SaaS product page
- "template" used positively — templates are what Brutalist is not

## Platform Registers

### Long-form essay
Lead with the medium argument. Establish the before/after in the first three paragraphs. Then earn the design argument by showing what goes wrong when access without design discipline produces the same bad habits in a more powerful medium.

### Video script
Open on the cargo cult belief — AI slides means smarter templates. Spend the first third dismantling it by demonstrating the medium. The analogy (chef vs. printed menu) carries the transition. Design argument in the final third.

### Short-form / social
One observation per post. The before/after fragments well:
- *Building a slide deck in code used to mean being a developer. That changed ten minutes ago.*
- *A browser doesn't display slides. It runs programs. PowerPoint never told you that.*
- *The deck reads itself aloud. It's a URL. It works on your phone. It's not a video.*

### The tool's own interactive voice
Terse, Socratic, mechanism-forward. Names what it's doing and why. Does not explain itself at length. Pushes back with a path forward. *That describes a mental state, not a behavior. What's the one thing they should be able to do?*

---

## The Test

Before publishing anything in the Brutalist voice, ask:

1. **Does it lead with the medium**, not with the pedagogy?
2. **Is the before/after legible** — does the reader understand what changed and why it matters?
3. **Is every abstract claim grounded** in a specific behavior, URL, or instance?
4. **Does it treat the reader as a non-developer** who now has access to a developer-grade medium?
5. **Does it end at the right place** — at the mechanism, not at inspiration?
